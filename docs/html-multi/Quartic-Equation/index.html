1.¬†Quartic Equation

This problem is stated in this youtube-video.

1.1.¬†Problem Statement

Find x \in \R that solves the quartic equation (x + 3) \cdot (x + 5) \cdot (x + 7) \cdot (x + 9) = 9

What immediately catches the eye is that the four factors on the left-hand side are evenly spaced around the midpoint x + 6. This symmetry suggests a substitution that can simplify the equation.

1.2.¬†Proof by Hand

This is actually the main idea behind the following proof.

\begin{align*} (x + 3) \cdot (x + 5) \cdot (x + 7) \cdot (x + 9) = 9 &\Longleftrightarrow \\ (t - 3) \cdot (t - 1) \cdot (t + 1) \cdot (t + 3) = 9 \;\; \text{by} \;\; t := x + 6 & \Longleftrightarrow \\ (t^2 - 3^2) \cdot (t^2 - 1^2) = 9 & \Longleftrightarrow \\ u^2 - 10 \cdot u + 9 = 9 \;\; \text{by} \;\; u := t^2 & \Longleftrightarrow \\ u \cdot (u - 10) = 0 & \Longleftrightarrow \\ u = 0 \, \lor \, u = 10 & \Longleftrightarrow \\ t = 0 \, \lor \, t = -\sqrt{10} \, \lor \, t = \sqrt{10} & \Longleftrightarrow \\ x = -6 \, \lor \, x = -6 -\sqrt{10} \, \lor \, t = -6 + \sqrt{10} & \\ \end{align*}

1.3.¬†Formal Proof in Lean

The proof is split into two parts. In the first part, it is shown that -6, -6 -\sqrt{10} and -6 +\sqrt{10} are solutions.

In the second part, it is shown that these are the only solutions.

This also follows from the fact that a quartic equation has at most four solutions and that the multiplicity of -6 as solution is two.

However, we rather go the hard way using only basic facts about real numbers.
